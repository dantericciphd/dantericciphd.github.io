Understanding PPMS

A Personal Guide for Audra's Primary Pulmonary Myxoid Sarcoma

What kind of tumor does Audra have, and how was it discovered? ▼

A 3-cm mass was discovered in Audra's lung, initially by X-ray, after an acute respiratory illness and persistent deep cough prompted a physician to order imaging. Follow-up CT and PET scans confirmed the mass was still present weeks after the illness resolved (although the cough lingered), leading to a series of diagnostic procedures including a biopsy of the mass itself via an outpatient bronchoscopy.

Expert pathologists at UCSF re-evaluated the biopsy samples after initial panels were inconclusive, performing genetic tests in addition to looking for certain cancer-specific tumor features. Genetic testing pointed to Primary Pulmonary Myxoid Sarcoma (PPMS), an exceptionally rare, low-grade tumor that develops in the lungs. Audra's tumor has the rare genetic EWSR1 rearrangement that is "pathognomonic" for PPMS (that is, it essentially rules out other cancer types with high certainty).

Key Characteristics of PPMS

  • Rarity: Only ~39 cases documented worldwide
  • Grade: Low-grade (slow-growing)
  • Location: Typically in lung airways or tissue
  • Origin: Arises from lung mesenchymal cells

Why the "Low-Grade" label Matters

Low-grade means the tumor cells divide slowly and behave less aggressively than high-grade cancers. This translates directly to better outcomes and more treatment options.

✓ Low-grade status = slower growth = better prognosis

What makes it "myxoid"?

Under the microscope, PPMS has a distinctive "jelly-like" (myxoid) appearance with delicate lace-like strands of spindle-shaped cells embedded in a gelatinous matrix. This unique pattern helps pathologists identify the tumor type, and significantly narrowed the list of possibilities in Audra's case.

Where do PPMS tumors come from? ▼

Risk Factors & Causes

PPMS does NOT result from smoking, environmental exposure, or anything Audra did or didn't do. It's a random genetic event, probably occurring in adulthood (i.e. not inherited), and could happen to anyone.

⚠ PPMS is not linked to smoking, age, gender, or lifestyle factors

Where did it come from?

PPMS arises from mesenchymal cells (structural cells) in the lung. A single cell experiences a chromosomal translocation—essentially a "typo" where parts of two chromosomes swap places—creating the EWSR1::CREB1 fusion gene.

This is a somatic mutation, meaning it happens only in that cell, not in Audra's entire body or inherited genes. It's not genetic in the hereditary sense—no family members are at increased risk.

Even with excellent prognosis, routine follow-up is important:

  • Regular imaging (chest X-rays and CT scans)
  • Typically: scans every 6 months for 2 years, then annually
  • Early detection of any recurrence allows prompt intervention

What will the surgery be like? ▼

Fortunately, Audra's tumor can be removed through minimally-invasive, video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery

VATS: What It Means

VATS = Video-Assisted Thoracoscopic Surgery

Instead of a large chest incision, the surgeon will make 2-4 small incisions between the ribs (about 1-2 cm each) and use a camera (thoracoscope) to guide the operation. Tools are inserted through these small ports.

✓ Minimally invasive = less pain, faster recovery, smaller scars

The Procedure Step-by-Step

  1. Positioning: Right side-lying (right lateral decubitus) to access left chest
  2. One-lung ventilation: Left lung is deflated so surgeon can work
  3. Vessel dissection: Pulmonary artery and vein to lower lobe are carefully identified and divided
  4. Bronchus division: The lower lobe airway is stapled
  5. Lobe removal: Entire left lower lobe containing tumor is removed
  6. Lymph node dissection: Regional lymph nodes examined and removed for staging
  7. Inspection: Remaining lung checked for air leaks and bleeding
  8. Closure: Chest tube placed; small incisions closed with sutures

Will she need chemo, radiation therapy, or immunotherapy? ▼

The Short Answer

Surgery is the primary treatment for PPMS. Current evidence does NOT support routine chemotherapy or radiation for PPMS after complete surgical resection.

✓ Audra most likely will NOT need chemotherapy or radiation

Why Surgery Alone?

  • Complete resection is curative: When the entire tumor is removed with clean margins (R0 resection), outcomes are excellent
  • Low-grade biology: Slow-growing tumors respond best to surgery; chemotherapy is less effective for low-grade disease
  • Evidence-based: All documented cases with good outcomes had surgery as the primary treatment
  • Safety: Avoiding unnecessary chemotherapy avoids side effects while maintaining cure rates

When Might Chemo Be Considered?

Chemotherapy might be considered if:

  • The tumor cannot be completely removed (R1/R2 resection)
  • The patient has metastatic (spread) disease at presentation
  • The tumor shows unusual aggressive features on pathology

None of these apply to Audra's case. Her tumor is localized, completely resectable, and shows typical low-grade features.

The Upshot

This is excellent news: months of chemotherapy aren't necessary, and Audra gets to focus on surgical recovery and a return to normal life.

Is there a chance the cancer could come back? ▼

The Risk is Low

Based on documented cases, ~85% of surgery patients have no recurrence. For Audra, this means the probability favors long-term disease-free survival.

Important Distinctions

Local recurrence (tumor growing back in the chest): Extremely rare after VATS lobectomy, because the entire lobe containing the tumor is removed.

Distant metastasis (spread to other organs): Possible, but fairly uncommon.

Risk Factors for Metastasis (Unclear)

Unlike some other cancers, PPMS prognostic factors are not yet fully understood. Current evidence suggests:

  • Tumor size may matter (smaller = lower risk)
  • Complete resection improves outcomes
  • EWSR1::CREB1 fusion presence alone does not predict recurrence
  • High proliferation rate (many mitoses) might suggest higher risk, but this is rare in PPMS

What to Do to Minimize Risk

  • Attend follow-up appointments: Regular imaging detects recurrence early
  • Report new symptoms: Unexplained cough, chest pain, shortness of breath warrant imaging
  • Maintain health: Exercise, healthy diet, avoid smoking (she's ahead here!)
  • Manage stress: Emotional wellness is part of recovery

The Reassuring Truth

85% of PPMS survivors achieve long-term disease-free status. Audra has many favorable factors pointing toward this disease-free 85% group. And, even if metastasis occurred, early detection through surveillance allows intervention.

✓ Probability favors very low chances of recurrence given Audra's profile
✓ Surveillance allows early detection and intervention if needed
For the nerds: How does the EWSR1::CREB1 genetic rearrangement lead to PPMS? ▼

The Chromosomal Swap

Normally, each cell has two copies of chromosome 2 and two copies of chromosome 22. In PPMS, a piece of chromosome 2 swaps with a piece of chromosome 22 at specific locations: t(2;22)(q33;q12).

This swap fuses the EWSR1 gene (from chr22) with the CREB1 gene (from chr2), creating a hybrid gene.

What's the Problem with This Fusion?

The fusion protein found in Audra's tumor is a "super-charged" version of the normal CREB1 protein:

  • EWSR1 portion: A powerful "transactivator" domain that cranks up gene activity
  • CREB1 portion: A DNA-binding domain that targets specific genes
  • Result: The fusion protein acts like a gas pedal stuck down on a specific set of genes

The Downstream Effects

This fusion protein drives abnormal expression of target genes that promote:

  • Cell proliferation (though slowly, due to low-grade biology)
  • Altered differentiation pathways
  • Changes in cell-cell adhesion
  • Myxoid matrix production (the "jelly-like" appearance)
✓ Despite driving the tumor, EWSR1::CREB1 actually promotes slower growth than other fusion proteins

Why Low-Grade Despite the Fusion?

Compared to other EWSR1 fusions (like EWSR1::FLI1 in Ewing sarcoma), the EWSR1::CREB1 fusion appears inherently less aggressive. This is why PPMS is reliably low-grade and why surgery is so effective.
